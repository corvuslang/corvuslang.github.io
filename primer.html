What the heck is a Corvus!?

An introduction to scripting with Corvus

Corvus is a friendly language for scripting just about anything. This guide introduces the language for those of us who aren't mega-programmers (yet üí™) with bite-sized examples.

By the way, the name "Corvus" comes from the family of birds that includes Crows and Ravens. They are highly intelligent, have adapted to a wide variety of climates, can work in groups, and are incredibly persistent. We hope Corvus inspires these same qualities.

Numbers

Ok, so... programming huh? Am I going to need maths for this?

Relax, there will be a little arithmetic, but we'll make the computer do it for us. First, let's get our bearings...

This guide is interactive. Wherever you see an example, the yellow box is a script and the grey box shows the result of running that script. When you can change the contents of the yellow box you will see results automatically. Your first script is just a single number: 42.

42

That's right, this script is yours now. Go ahead and change the number, the result will change with it. If you change the script to something that isn't a number, like "cheese grater", you might see an error: the grey box will turn pink and show an error message. Computers are quite dull compared to people, they don't interrupt us with juicy gossip when we call, just give us the boring answer straight away: "forty-two", click. üòë ... but that's also why we love them.

Just like the calculator you had in school (or the calculator app on your phone), calc can do more than add. You can ask calc to do more calculations by adding more keywords to the call. In addition (üò¨) to plus, calc understands the keywords subtract, times, and dividedBy. Just like a pocket calculator, it performs these operations in the order they are written, here's a longer example to demonstrate:

calc: 10 times: 2 plus: 1 dividedBy: 3 times: 6

More functions and variables

You've mastered calc, but having just one function is a lot like having a phone that only calls one person, so let's expand our repertoire (or is it an entourage? ü§î).

Next up we're going to ask the computer another easy question: is one number bigger than another?

compare: 10 biggerThan: 20

Why bother asking the computer a question with an obvious answer? To answer that we need to introduce variables. This next example shows a new capability: if you write a word (like rewardPoints), Corvus assumes that it refers to a number that comes from outside your script.

compare: rewardPoints biggerThan: 20
